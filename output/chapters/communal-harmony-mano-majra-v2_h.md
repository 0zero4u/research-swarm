# Communal Harmony in Mano Majra

## Pre-Partition Life: A Portrait of Coexistence

Mano Majra, the village at the center of Khushwant Singh's *Train to Pakistan*, is home to Sikhs and Muslims who live together as landowners and sharecroppers in conditions Singh calls "simple comradeship" (Charyulu 2019, p. 1). The phrase is deliberate. Singh is not idealizing what he saw, or sentimentalizing it. He is simply stating a fact: Sikhs and Muslims in this village got along, and the reason had something to do with the fact that their daily lives intersected through work rather than through religious identity. Class relations, the novel suggests, shaped daily interactions more immediately than faith did.

The railway station anchors this communal life. Singh describes it as one of the town's habitats (Charyulu 2019, p. 2), placing it in the same category as homes and fields. Women talk there; children play nearby. The station is a public space where residents of different faiths encounter one another without obvious friction, and the trains arrive on a schedule reliable enough to structure daily life around.

## The Arrival of Violence: Disruption of Harmony

The peace of Mano Majra does not survive the regional upheaval that comes with Partition. Singh writes plainly that this peace is "before long upset by savagery on a lot more prominent scope" (Charyulu 2019, p. 1), placing what happens in the village squarely within a larger pattern of violence across the subcontinent. What follows is not a gradual unraveling but a sudden rupture. Two trainloads of corpses arrive in the village. The townspeople see these bodies and learn that Sikhs and Hindus are being massacred in Pakistan (Charyulu 2019, p. 1). They learn of the violence through the evidence of corpses, not through news reports. This is a reversal: the railway that once connected the village to the world now delivers the evidence of its own community's destruction.

The disruption extends to the railway's social function. The trains become "less prompt than at any other time" (Charyulu 2019, p. 2), a plain observation that carries the weight of institutional collapse. A community that depended on the railway's rhythm for social life now finds even that basic regularity broken. More significantly, the Muslims of the town are "gathered together and cleared for their own wellbeing" (Charyulu 2019, p. 1), an evacuation that disperses the communal life the novel has just described. The village empties of one faith group, leaving the remaining Sikhs and Hindus to face the immediate threat of massacre. The language Singh uses here is restrained: "cleared" domesticates a traumatic evacuation, and "wellbeing" implies survival rather than safety. The pre-Partition harmony of Mano Majra survives in memory only, as the village becomes a place defined by fear rather than comradeship.
