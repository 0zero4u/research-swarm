# Ghost Train Symbolism in *Train to Pakistan*

## Introduction

The train becomes the dominant symbol in Khushwant Singh's *Train to Pakistan* (1956). What starts as an ordinary feature of rural life turns into an instrument of communal annihilation during the Partition of 1947. Scholars have traced the rail route as a central motif through which Singh examines how ordinary spaces become sites of collective violence (Unknown 1947, p. 1). This section investigates the spectral dimension of the train motif. I argue that the "ghost train" represents the failure of traumatized memory to process the scale of loss that Partition inflicted on Punjabi society.

## The Railroad as Site of Horror

Mano Majra sits at a railroad intersection, a geographical fact that shapes its fate during the communal riots of 1947 (Unknown 1947, p. 1). The train does not merely pass through the village; it brings death in concentrated form. As Charyulu notes, the town becomes "the focal point of collective scorn just when the trains arrive with their cargoes of dead" (Unknown 1947, p. 1). The rail line functions as a literal and symbolic pathway between communities that had previously lived alongside each other without obvious friction. When trains carrying dead bodies start arriving, the intersection becomes a place where people witness, and participate in, systematic violence. Characters in the novel exist as witnesses to this spectacle, and their responses—whether horror, complicity, or paralysis—reveal the moral fragmentation that Partition produced (Unknown 1947, p. 1).

## Spectral Presence and Collective Memory

The ghost train operates on multiple levels. First, it appears as a literal presence: trains arrive bearing corpses, yet the mechanisms of violence remain beyond rational explanation. Second, the ghost train haunts the collective memory of the community, surfacing in the narrative as an event that residents cannot adequately comprehend or articulate. Gupta observes that Singh presents his characters as "desolate people, who suffer not only from inhumane treatment but from the sacrifice of people for their nation" (Unknown 1947, p. 1). This double suffering—the personal and the collective—requires a symbolic register that exceeds realistic representation.

The spectral quality of the train emerges from Singh's narrative technique. Rather than explaining Partition violence through political analysis, Singh allows the train to appear and reappear as an uncanny interruption of ordinary life. The ghost train does not communicate historical causes; it embodies the failure of language and memory to contain what happened. Badiger's comparative study of Singh alongside Bapsi Sidhwa places both writers within a tradition of Partition literature that uses symbolic structures to address the unspeakable (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india, Unknown, p. 1). In this tradition, the ghost train figures the trauma that exceeds representation.

## Conclusion

The ghost train in *Train to Pakistan* examines how Partition violence permeated the physical and psychological landscape of Punjabi villages. Through the recurring motif of the railroad intersection and its cargoes of death, Singh builds a symbol that encodes both the historical reality of 1947 and the inability of narrative to convey its full horror. The ghost train, in this reading, is not a supernatural element. It figures the persistent, unprocessed nature of Partition trauma within South Asian collective memory.
