## The Ghost Train as Symbol of Partition Violence in *Train to Pakistan*

### The Railway as Narrative Axis

The railway sits at the center of Khushwant Singh's *Train to Pakistan*, the main structure through which Partition violence becomes tangible in the narrative. Mano Majra, a small fictional village right on the India-Pakistan border, functions as the setting for this examination of communal catastrophe. The railroad crossing is where both peace and destruction move through [(363508_8461703, Charyulu 2019, p. 1)].

Scholars note that Mano Majra has "the sad differentiation of being a railroad intersection," though it holds "no political or military significance" [(363508_8461703, Charyulu 2019, p. 1)]. This unremarkable location makes the village useful for studying how ordinary rural communities got caught up in sectarian violence. The railroad is "one of the town habitats, where women gather to talk and kids to play," showing how central this infrastructure was in everyday Punjabi village life [(363508_8461703, Charyulu 2019, p. 2)].

### The Transformation of the Railroad

The arrival of the ghost trains changes everything. When trains start coming in with "their grievous cargoes of death," the railroad is no longer a place for daily activity. It becomes something that signals catastrophe [(363508_8461703, Charyulu 2019, p. 2)]. Two trainloads of ravaged bodies arrive in Mano Majra, events that reveal "to the frightened townspeople that the Sikhs and Hindus are being slaughtered in Pakistan" [(363508_8461703, Charyulu 2019, p. 1)].
