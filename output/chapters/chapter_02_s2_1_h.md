## 2.1 Scholarship on Partition Literature

The scholarly examination of Partition literature has changed significantly since the 1990s, moving from predominantly political and historiographical approaches toward methodologies that focus on personal testimony and affective experience. This shift has changed how literary critics engage with Partition narratives, reshaping both the canonical texts available for study and the critical frameworks applied to them. Understanding this evolution is essential for any comparative analysis of Khushwant Singh's "Train to Pakistan" and its cinematic adaptation, as the novel occupies an important position within this changing landscape of Partition scholarship.

Urvashi Butalia's "The Other Side of Silence: Voices from the Partition of India" (2000) marks a methodological watershed in Partition studies, establishing oral history as a legitimate and rigorous approach to understanding the catastrophe (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india.txt, p. 26). Butalia explicitly articulated the rationale for prioritising personal testimony over institutional history, arguing that "it is not only the 'facts' of any event that are important, but equally, how people remember those facts, and how they represent them" (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india.txt, p. 26). This formulation rejected the hierarchy that placed political history above personal narrative, instead treating memory work as equally valid scholarly evidence. The implications for literary study were considerable, as scholars increasingly recognised that fictional representations of Partition could access dimensions of experience that official histories deliberately obscured or minimised.

Butalia described her methodology in terms that distinguished it sharply from conventional historical research. She employed "no questionnaire, no chosen 'sample' of people, in terms of geography or class or any other category," instead allowing interviewees to "speak about that time in their lives" while permitting "the conversation take its own course" (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india.txt, p. 36). This non-directive approach reflected a theoretical position that valued individual agency in remembrance over scholarly control of narrative shape. Butalia further maintained that she did "not believe the transcript of any interview can ever be an unmediated text," acknowledging the inevitably constructed nature of all testimonial accounts (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india.txt, p. 34). Such methodological transparency distinguished her work from earlier Partition histories that claimed objectivity while suppressing inconvenient testimonies.

The oral history methodology brought particular attention to voices that had remained silent within mainstream narratives. Butalia observed that recovering women's voices proved especially difficult, requiring researchers "to begin to pose different questions, to talk in different situations, and to be prepared to do that most important of things, to listen: to their speech, their silences, the half-said things, the nuances" (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india.txt, p. 114). This attention to silence as meaningful speech marked a significant departure from historians who had treated absence of testimony as evidence of nothing beyond absence. The turn toward affect and emotion, sometimes termed the "affective turn" in Partition studies, generated new frameworks for understanding how literary texts engage with historical trauma (27.-Ishika-Kundra-and-Dr.-Sagar-Kumar-Sharma, Kundra and Sharma 2025, p. 2).

Scholarship on Partition literature has consistently recognised the inadequacy of purely political frameworks for understanding the catastrophe's human dimensions. Butalia noted that Partition was "surely more than just a political divide, or a division of properties, of assets and liabilities. It was also, to use a phrase that survivors use repeatedly, a 'division of hearts'" (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india.txt, p. 25). This characterisation emphasised the emotional and psychological scarring that persisted long after the political settlement had been established, providing literary critics with theoretical grounds for examining how texts represent trauma beyond immediate violence. The historiographical tradition preceding Butalia had prioritised State-level narratives and official documentation, treating individual testimonies as supplementary rather than primary evidence. Butalia complicated this hierarchy by distinguishing between "memories of the State, professional, or historical memory" and "the memories of survivors, whether they are victims or aggressors or both" (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india.txt, p. 293). This categorisation did not privilege one memory type over another but rather recognised each as offering distinct evidentiary value. Literary critics subsequently drew upon this framework to analyse how novels and films engage with different memory types, examining the formal strategies through which personal testimony could be represented, contested, or silenced within narrative structures.

Scholarship on "Train to Pakistan" has benefited from these methodological developments, as Singh's novel highlights precisely the tensions between official history and personal experience that oral history approaches seek to address. Critics have examined how the text navigates the challenge of representing trauma while avoiding both sentimentality and detachment (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india.txt, p. 29). The novel's focus on ordinary villagers rather than political actors aligns with the oral history emphasis on subaltern perspectives, though Singh's narrative stance differs markedly from Butalia's documentary methodology. This distinction is significant for comparative analysis of the novel and film, as the adaptation must translate literary strategies of trauma representation into cinematic equivalents.

The affective turn in Partition studies has sparked debate regarding the relationship between historical accuracy and emotional truth. Butalia observed that memory itself is "shifting, changing, unreliable" yet argued for examining "the memories for themselves" rather than treating them primarily as sources of factual verification (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india.txt, p. 29). Literary critics have applied similar principles to fictional texts, evaluating representations not by their documentary fidelity but by their capacity to communicate the texture of lived experience under conditions of extreme violence. This framework is particularly relevant for analysing "Train to Pakistan," where the novel's apparent simplicity of language has been read as both aesthetic choice and ethical position (Train-To-Pakistan.txt, p. 168).

Scholarly approaches to Partition literature have also engaged with questions of communal identity and belonging, examining how texts represent the social categories through which violence was enacted and experienced. Research has shown how characters in "Train to Pakistan" navigate religious affiliations that become simultaneously sources of identity and categories of violence (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india.txt, p. 1). The recognition that communal hatred is "not natural but socially and politically constructed" has informed critical readings that examine the novel's socialisation of violence rather than treating it as inevitable or essential (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india.txt, p. 30). Such approaches draw upon broader developments in Partition scholarship that emphasise the politically mediated nature of communal conflict rather than attributing violence to ancient enmities.

The evolution of Partition literature scholarship has moved through several phases: from political history to oral testimony, from official to personal narratives, from facts to memories, from event to affect. This trajectory has created space for literary analysis that can address the catastrophic dimensions of Partition through attention to formal strategies, emotional registers, and testimonial structures. Singh's "Train to Pakistan" occupies a significant position within this scholarship, serving as both product of its historical moment and subject of ongoing critical reassessment as new methodological approaches emerge.
