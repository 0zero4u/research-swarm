# Chapter I: Introduction

## 1.1 Partition of India

The Partition of British India in 1947 was one of the most traumatic events in modern South Asian history. The division of the subcontinent into two independent dominions, India and Pakistan, was accompanied by violence, displacement, and communal hatred that affected millions. Urvashi Butalia, in her seminal oral history of the period, notes that "the statistics are staggering. Twelve million people were displaced; a million died; seventy-five thousand women were said to have been abducted and raped; families were divided; properties lost; homes destroyed" [B_001]. These figures alone convey the magnitude of a human catastrophe that continues to reverberate through the cultural and political memory of both nations.

The immediate historical cause of Partition lay in the British colonial decision to divide the territory along religious lines before withdrawing from the subcontinent. As Darlami Magar observes, "In 1947 the British government divided India into two halves: India and Pakistan before leaving India. It caused violence, separation, and displacement among people living in the contemporary harmonious community of Hindu, Muslim, Shikh, and Christian in India" [B_002]. The division was predicated on the two-nation theory, which held that Hindus and Muslims constituted distinct nations incapable of coexistence within a single state. This political doctrine, championed by the All-India Muslim League under Muhammad Ali Jinnah, became a violent reality in the Radcliffe Line, which demarcated the boundaries of the two new states.

The province of Punjab, situated along the newly drawn border, experienced the most intense violence. Ahmad documents that "In 1947, when the new country Pakistan was formally announced, about ten millions people – Hindu, Muslims and Sikhs were shifting their place of living. They collided with each other and there started communal riots which led to the killing of thousands of innocent people of both the sides" [B_003]. The violence was neither spontaneous nor one-sided. It involved organized massacres, retaliatory killings, and systemic atrocities committed by all communities. Shovon notes that "In August 1947, two countries got their independence: India and Pakistan, which were created on the basis of the religious majority in each part. The following days saw one of the biggest migrations of human history... Stories of murder, rape, beating, forced conversion, kidnapping, and property grabbing emerged in various corners, especially in the frontier zones" [B_004].

Women were particularly targeted during Partition violence. They became targets of sexual violence as a means of communal dishonor and territorial claim. Official estimates suggest that between twenty-five thousand and twenty-nine thousand Sikh and Hindu women, and another twelve thousand to nineteen thousand Muslim women, were subjected to torture, rape, forced marriage, conversion, and abduction, though local sources claimed the numbers were higher [B_004]. Many women were killed by their own family members to preserve community honor, while others were abducted and forcibly converted. The trauma of these events was not limited to the immediate aftermath; it persisted across generations through silence, memory, and narrative.

The literary response to Partition has been extensive. Novels, short stories, poetry, and memoirs have sought to capture the human dimensions of a tragedy that official histories often reduce to political decisions and demographic statistics. This dissertation focuses on one of the earliest and most significant literary engagements with Partition: Khushwant Singh's *Train to Pakistan* (1956), a novel that dramatizes the collapse of communal harmony through the microcosm of a single village.

## 1.2 About Khushwant Singh

Khushwant Singh (1915–2014) was a prolific and controversial writer in modern Indian literature. Born on August 14, 1915, in Hadali, a small town in what is now Pakistan, Singh belonged to a Sikh family with a long tradition of military and administrative service to the British Raj [B_005]. His early education at Government College, Lahore, and subsequent law degree from King's College, London, gave him a cosmopolitan intellectual foundation that shaped his literary and journalistic career.

Singh's professional life was remarkably varied. Before establishing himself as a writer, he practiced law in Lahore, served as an Information and Mass Communication Officer in the Indian Foreign Service, worked with UNESCO, and held editorial positions at major Indian newspapers [B_008]. This breadth of experience gave him a unique vantage point from which to observe and critique the social and political transformations of post-independence India. His journalistic career included founding and editing *Yojana*, serving as editor of the *Illustrated Weekly of India*, the *National Herald*, and the *Hindustan Times* [B_006].

Singh's literary output was equally diverse. His first published work, *The Mark of Vishnu and Other Stories* (1950), established him as a writer who explored complex human relationships with wit and candor [B_006]. His first novel, *Train to Pakistan* (1956), secured his place in the canon of Indian English literature. The novel emerged from his personal witness to the Partition violence and his desire to record the tragedy without romanticization. Charyulu notes that "Khushwant Singh turns a tenacious eye on complex responses to such catastrophes as the Second World War and the wicked segment of India and Pakistan. To these occasions he brings objectivity and separation; he isn't one to compliment or to romanticize" [B_007].

Singh's themes included Partition and communalism, the complexities of human relationships, and the contradictions of Indian society [B_006]. His writing was characterized by simplicity, directness, humor, and an unflinching willingness to address taboo subjects. Beyond fiction, he produced significant non-fiction works, including the two-volume *A History of the Sikhs* (1963), which remains an essential reference for scholars of Sikh history and culture, and his autobiography *Truth, Love and a Little Malice* (2002) [B_006].

Throughout his career, Singh received numerous accolades, including the Padma Bhushan in 1974, which he returned in 1984 in protest against the Indian Army's storming of the Golden Temple, and the Padma Vibhushan in 2007 [B_005]. He passed away on March 20, 2014, leaving behind a literary legacy that continues to shape discussions of Indian identity, history, and memory.

## 1.3 About the novel *Train to Pakistan*

*Train to Pakistan* is set in the fictional village of Mano Majra, located on the border between India and Pakistan, near the Sutlej River in Punjab [B_010]. The village is small, comprising only about seventy families, with Sikhs and Muslims living in roughly equal numbers, alongside a single Hindu moneylender family and a few sweepers of uncertain religious affiliation [B_011]. Before the arrival of Partition violence, Mano Majra is depicted as a place of communal harmony. The villagers share a common deity, participate in each other's daily rhythms, and maintain cooperative economic relationships: "The Sikhs own all the land around the village; the Muslims are tenants and share the tilling with the owners" [B_010].

The narrative centers on three principal characters. Iqbal Singh, a young urban-educated communist, arrives in the village as a social worker sent by his party to mediate communal tensions [B_011]. Juggut Singh, a village gangster with a criminal record, embodies the raw physical vitality of rural India and maintains a secret relationship with Nooran, a Muslim girl [B_009]. Hukum Chand, the district magistrate, represents the corruption and moral paralysis of the colonial administrative apparatus [B_009].

The plot unfolds with the murder of Lala Ram Lal, the Hindu moneylender, by a gang of dacoits led by Malli. Juggut Singh is falsely arrested as a suspect due to his criminal reputation and his affair with Nooran [B_011]. The arrival of a "ghost train" from Pakistan loaded with the corpses of murdered Sikhs shatters the village's equilibrium and triggers a spiral of communal retribution [B_012]. The local authorities, unable or unwilling to prevent violence, decide to evacuate the Muslim residents to refugee camps. The novel culminates in Juggut Singh's sacrifice: learning that a group of Sikh militants plans to attack the train carrying the Muslim refugees, including Nooran, he single-handedly attempts to cut the rope blocking the tracks and dies under the train's wheels, allowing the refugees to pass safely to Pakistan [B_012].

The novel is thematically complex. The railroad is a central symbol that represents both the connectivity of pre-Partition life and the instrument of death and division [B_012]. Charyulu observes that "The rail route in Train to Pakistan has especially fascinating and vile job... The railroad is a self-evident yet profoundly powerful image of India's misfortune" [B_012]. The disruption of the train schedule mirrors the disruption of social order: "Trains turned out to be less prompt than at any other time and a lot more began to go through around evening time" [B_012].

The theme of identity and belonging is central to the novel. Saeed, Shafi, and Masood argue that "religious, cultural, and national affiliations significantly influence the characters' identities and sense of belonging" [B_014]. The study applies Social Identity Theory to demonstrate how external categorizations lead to stereotypes and prejudice, while characters also exhibit agency in negotiating their identities [B_014]. Shovon adds that "Singh presents Mano Majra, which is intended as a microcosm of India as an archetype of secular heaven where people of diverse religions live in a harmonious coexistence" [B_015]. The collapse of this harmony dramatizes the broader national tragedy.

Other significant themes include the critique of administrative corruption, the failure of political ideology to prevent violence, and the gendered dimensions of communal conflict. The novel does not present Partition as an abstract political event but as a lived catastrophe that transforms individual relationships and collective identities.

## 1.4 About the film adaptation

The cinematic adaptation of *Train to Pakistan* was released in 1998, directed by Pamela Rooks [B_016]. The film translates Singh's literary vision into visual form, using an ensemble cast including Nirmal Pandey, Rajit Kapur, Mohan Agashe, Smriti Mishra, Mangal Dhillon, and Divya Dutta to portray the novel's complex social milieu [B_018]. The adaptation presented significant challenges, as the novel's power derives not only from its plot but from its evocative descriptions of rural Punjab, the atmospheric presence of the railway, and the gradual erosion of communal trust.

Pawar and Al-Qadhi note that "Khushwant Singh... wrote his marvelous fiction Train to Pakistan in 1956 but it was adapted into the cinematic and visual world in 1998 and was directed by Pamela Rooks" [B_016]. The filmmakers constructed an imaginative village to represent Mano Majra, populating it with the same religious and social diversity described in the novel: "Rooks and her fellow-actors decided to create an imaginative village including the same features (trees, poor peasants, railway station, pastoral cottages, homes of worship, pets, and two paramours)" [B_018]. This attention to material detail was essential for conveying the pre-Partition atmosphere of harmony that makes the subsequent violence so devastating.

The adaptation also grappled with the challenge of representing the evacuation chaos that formed the historical backdrop of the narrative. Pawar and Al-Qadhi argue that "the videoing adaptation of the literary oeuvre can carry the fundamental theme in more apparent method than the written feat" [B_017]. The visual medium allows for the direct depiction of crowds, refugee columns, and the physical devastation of Partition, elements that Singh could only describe through prose. The film necessarily condenses and simplifies the novel's interior monologues and ideological debates, particularly the reflections of Iqbal Singh and Hukum Chand.

The dissertation will engage with both the novel and the film adaptation as complementary texts that illuminate different dimensions of the Partition experience. While the novel offers psychological depth and symbolic complexity, the film provides visual immediacy and affective power. A comparative analysis of the two texts reveals how medium-specific conventions shape the representation of historical trauma.

## 1.5 Aim and objectives

This dissertation examines how Khushwant Singh's *Train to Pakistan* represents the trauma, violence, and communal disintegration caused by the Partition of 1947, and analyzes how the 1998 film adaptation by Pamela Rooks reinterprets these themes for a visual medium. The study moves beyond surface-level plot analysis to investigate the novel's symbolic architecture, its critique of nationalism and administrative failure, and its portrayal of gendered violence.

The specific objectives are as follows:

First, to analyze the historical context of the 1947 Partition as depicted in the novel, focusing on the transformation of Mano Majra from a site of communal harmony to a space of sectarian violence.

Second, to examine the novel's major themes, including the symbolism of the train, the construction and dissolution of religious identity, the failure of political and legal institutions, and the gendered dimensions of Partition violence.

Third, to evaluate the characterization of Iqbal Singh, Juggut Singh, and Hukum Chand as representatives of different responses to the moral crisis of Partition.

Fourth, to compare the novel with its 1998 film adaptation, analyzing how the shift from literary to cinematic medium affects the representation of historical trauma, communal identity, and individual sacrifice.

Fifth, to assess the novel's contribution to Partition literature and its ongoing relevance to discussions of communalism, nationalism, and historical memory in South Asia.

## 1.6 Research methodology and scope

This study uses a qualitative research methodology based on close reading and thematic analysis of the primary texts [B_019]. The approach is interdisciplinary, drawing on literary criticism, film studies, and historical analysis to situate *Train to Pakistan* within the broader field of Partition literature. The theoretical framework incorporates Gyanendra Pandey's work on the historiography of Partition violence and trauma, which provides a critical lens for understanding how the novel negotiates between individual experience and collective catastrophe [B_020].

The primary textual materials consist of Khushwant Singh's novel *Train to Pakistan* (1956) and Pamela Rooks's film adaptation (1998). These texts are analyzed through a comparative framework that attends to both their thematic continuities and their medium-specific differences. The novel is read as a historical document that bears witness to the atrocities of 1947, while the film is examined as a visual reinterpretation that uses cinematic techniques to evoke affective responses.

Secondary sources include scholarly articles and books on Partition literature, Sikh history, and the politics of communal identity. Joshi's analysis of affective politics in Partition novels provides a methodological point of departure: "This paper analyzes selected Indian partition novels to unravel affective politics underlined in them. The major affects underlying in these novels are those of love, hatred, happiness, unhappiness, and outrage" [B_021]. This dissertation extends such analyses by examining how affect operates across both literary and cinematic texts.

The scope of the study is limited to Singh's novel and Rooks's film. While other Partition texts, such as Bhisham Sahni's *Tamas* and Salman Rushdie's *Midnight's Children*, offer valuable comparative contexts, the focus on *Train to Pakistan* allows for a sustained analysis of how a single narrative negotiates the complexities of Partition experience. The study does not claim to provide a comprehensive history of the Partition but rather to illuminate how literature and film mediate historical trauma.

## 1.7 Thesis statement

This dissertation argues that Khushwant Singh's *Train to Pakistan* uses the microcosm of Mano Majra to expose the fragility of communal harmony when religious identity is instrumentalized for political ends. Through its symbolic use of the railroad, its characterization of Iqbal Singh, Juggut Singh, and Hukum Chand, and its depiction of administrative complicity and gendered violence, the novel shows that Partition was not merely a political division but a moral and social rupture that destroyed the possibility of coexistence. The 1998 film adaptation by Pamela Rooks reinterprets this narrative for a visual medium, translating the novel's symbolic density into cinematic immediacy and making the trauma of Partition accessible to new audiences. By analyzing both texts, this study contends that the representation of Partition in literature and film serves as both historical documentation and an ongoing intervention into the politics of memory, identity, and belonging in South Asia.
