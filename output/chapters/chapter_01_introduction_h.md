# Chapter I: Introduction

## 1.1 Partition of India

In 1947, British India split into two independent dominions, India and Pakistan. The violence, displacement, and communal hatred that followed affected millions. Urvashi Butalia, in her oral history of the period, notes that "the statistics are staggering. Twelve million people were displaced; a million died; seventy-five thousand women were said to have been abducted and raped; families were divided; properties lost; homes destroyed" (Partition_The_Holocaust_Train_to_Pakista, Unknown, p. 1). These figures alone convey the magnitude of a human catastrophe that continues to reverberate through the cultural and political memory of both nations.

The British decided to divide the territory along religious lines before withdrawing from the subcontinent. Darlami Magar writes that "In 1947 the British government divided India into two halves: India and Pakistan before leaving India. It caused violence, separation, and displacement among people living in the contemporary harmonious community of Hindu, Muslim, Shikh, and Christian in India" (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india, Unknown, p. 1). The division grew from the two-nation theory, which held that Hindus and Muslims constituted distinct nations incapable of coexistence within a single state. The All-India Muslim League under Muhammad Ali Jinnah championed this doctrine, and it became a violent reality in the Radcliffe Line that demarcated the boundaries of the two new states.

No province suffered more intensely than Punjab, which sat directly on the new border. Ahmad documents that "In 1947, when the new country Pakistan was formally announced, about ten millions people – Hindu, Muslims and Sikhs were shifting their place of living. They collided with each other and there started communal riots which led to the killing of thousands of innocent people of both the sides" (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india, Unknown, p. 1). The violence was neither spontaneous nor one-sided. It involved organized massacres, retaliatory killings, and systemic atrocities committed by all communities. Shovon notes that "In August 1947, two countries got their independence: India and Pakistan, which were created on the basis of the religious majority in each part. The following days saw one of the biggest migrations of human history... Stories of murder, rape, beating, forced conversion, kidnapping, and property grabbing emerged in various corners, especially in the frontier zones" (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india, Unknown, p. 1).

Sexual violence became a weapon of communal terror directed mainly at women. Official estimates suggest that between twenty-five thousand and twenty-nine thousand Sikh and Hindu women, and another twelve thousand to nineteen thousand Muslim women, endured torture, rape, forced marriage, conversion, and abduction, though local sources claimed the numbers were higher (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india, Unknown, p. 1). Many women were killed by their own family members to preserve community honor, while others were abducted and forcibly converted. The trauma of these events was not limited to the immediate aftermath; it persisted across generations through silence, memory, and narrative.

Novels, short stories, poetry, and memoirs have sought to capture the human dimensions of a tragedy that official histories often reduce to political decisions and demographic statistics. I examine one of the earliest and most significant literary engagements with Partition: Khushwant Singh's *Train to Pakistan* (1956), a novel that dramatizes the collapse of communal harmony through the microcosm of a single village.

## 1.2 About Khushwant Singh

Khushwant Singh was born on August 14, 1915, in Hadali, a small town in what is now Pakistan. He belonged to a Sikh family with a long tradition of military and administrative service to the British Raj (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india, Unknown, p. 1). His early education at Government College, Lahore, and subsequent law degree from King's College, London, gave him a cosmopolitan intellectual foundation that shaped his literary and journalistic career.

Singh established himself as a writer before he became a journalist. His first published work, *The Mark of Vishnu and Other Stories* (1950), explored complex human relationships with wit and candor (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india, Unknown, p. 1). His first novel, *Train to Pakistan* (1956), secured his place in the canon of Indian English literature. The novel emerged from his personal witness to the Partition violence and his desire to record the tragedy without romanticization. Charyulu notes that "Khushwant Singh turns a tenacious eye on complex responses to such catastrophes as the Second World War and the wicked segment of India and Pakistan. To these occasions he brings objectivity and separation; he isn't one to compliment or to romanticize" (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india, Unknown, p. 1).

Before turning to journalism, he practiced law in Lahore, served as an Information and Mass Communication Officer in the Indian Foreign Service, worked with UNESCO, and held editorial positions at major Indian newspapers (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india, Unknown, p. 1). This breadth of experience gave him a unique vantage point from which to observe and critique the social and political transformations of post-independence India. His journalistic career included founding and editing *Yojana*, serving as editor of the *Illustrated Weekly of India*, the *National Herald*, and the *Hindustan Times* (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india, Unknown, p. 1).

Partition and communalism, the contradictions of Indian society, and the complexities of human relationships run through his writing (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india, Unknown, p. 1). His prose used simplicity, directness, humor, and an unflinching willingness to address taboo subjects. Beyond fiction, he produced significant non-fiction works, including the two-volume *A History of the Sikhs* (1963), which remains an essential reference for scholars of Sikh history and culture, and his autobiography *Truth, Love and a Little Malice* (2002) (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india, Unknown, p. 1).

Throughout his career, Singh received numerous accolades, including the Padma Bhushan in 1974, which he returned in 1984 in protest against the Indian Army's storming of the Golden Temple, and the Padma Vibhushan in 2007 (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india, Unknown, p. 1). He passed away on March 20, 2014, leaving behind a literary legacy that continues to shape discussions of Indian identity, history, and memory.

## 1.3 About the novel *Train to Pakistan*

Mano Majra, the fictional village where *Train to Pakistan* unfolds, sits on the border between India and Pakistan near the Sutlej River in Punjab (IdentityandBelonging_AnalysisofTraintoPakistan, (Lecturer and and 2023, p. 1). The village is small, comprising only about seventy families, with Sikhs and Muslims living in roughly equal numbers, alongside a single Hindu moneylender family and a few sweepers of uncertain religious affiliation B_011. Before the arrival of Partition violence, Mano Majra is depicted as a place of communal harmony. The villagers share a common deity, participate in each other's daily rhythms, and maintain cooperative economic relationships: "The Sikhs own all the land around the village; the Muslims are tenants and share the tilling with the owners" (IdentityandBelonging_AnalysisofTraintoPakistan, (Lecturer and and 2023, p. 1).

Three characters dominate the narrative. Iqbal Singh, a young urban-educated communist, arrives in the village as a social worker sent by his party to mediate communal tensions B_011. Juggut Singh, a village gangster with a criminal record, embodies the raw physical vitality of rural India and maintains a secret relationship with Nooran, a Muslim girl (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india, Unknown, p. 1). Hukum Chand, the district magistrate, represents the corruption and moral paralysis of the colonial administrative apparatus (the-other-side-of-silence-voices-from-the-partition-of-india, Unknown, p. 1).

The plot begins with the murder of Lala Ram Lal, the Hindu moneylender, by a gang of dacoits led by Malli. Juggut Singh is falsely arrested as a suspect due to his criminal reputation and his affair with Nooran B_011. The arrival of a "ghost train" from Pakistan loaded with the corpses of murdered Sikhs shatters the village's equilibrium and triggers a spiral of communal retribution B_012. The local authorities, unable or unwilling to prevent violence, decide to evacuate the Muslim residents to refugee camps. The novel culminates in Juggut Singh's sacrifice: learning that a group of Sikh militants plans to attack the train carrying the Muslim refugees, including Nooran, he single-handedly attempts to cut the rope blocking the tracks and dies under the train's wheels, allowing the refugees to pass safely to Pakistan B_012.

The railroad runs through the novel as both a connection and a weapon. For Charyulu, it has "especially fascinating and vile job... The railroad is a self-evident yet profoundly powerful image of India's misfortune" B_012. The disruption of the train schedule mirrors the disruption of social order: "Trains turned out to be less prompt than at any other time and a lot more began to go through around evening time" B_012.

Questions of who belongs where drive the story. Saeed, Shafi, and Masood argue that "religious, cultural, and national affiliations significantly influence the characters' identities and sense of belonging" B_014. Social Identity Theory helps show how outside categories create stereotypes and prejudice, but the characters also push back and negotiate their identities B_014. Shovon adds that "Singh presents Mano Majra, which is intended as a microcosm of India as an archetype of secular heaven where people of diverse religions live in a harmonious coexistence" B_015. The collapse of this harmony dramatizes the broader national tragedy.

Other significant themes include the critique of administrative corruption, the failure of political ideology to prevent violence, and the gendered dimensions of communal conflict. The novel does not present Partition as an abstract political event but as a lived catastrophe that transforms individual relationships and collective identities.

## 1.4 About the film adaptation

Pamela Rooks directed the 1998 film adaptation B_016. The cast — Nirmal Pandey, Rajit Kapur, Mohan Agashe, Smriti Mishra, Mangal Dhillon, Divya Dutta — brought the novel's social world to the screen B_018. The challenge was the same one any Partition adaptation faces: how to show violence that was not just physical but social, a breakdown of trust rather than just a sequence of events.

Creating the village on screen required careful attention to material detail. Pawar and Al-Qadhi note that "Khushwant Singh... wrote his marvelous fiction Train to Pakistan in 1956 but it was adapted into the cinematic and visual world in 1998 and was directed by Pamela Rooks" B_016. The filmmakers constructed an imaginative village to represent Mano Majra, populating it with the same religious and social diversity described in the novel: "Rooks and her fellow-actors decided to create an imaginative village including the same features (trees, poor peasants, railway station, pastoral cottages, homes of worship, pets, and two paramours)" B_018. This attention to material detail was essential for conveying the pre-Partition atmosphere of harmony that makes the subsequent violence so devastating.

The film also had to visualize the evacuation chaos that formed the historical backdrop of the narrative. Pawar and Al-Qadhi argue that "the videoing adaptation of the literary oeuvre can carry the fundamental theme in more apparent method than the written feat" B_017. The visual medium allows for the direct depiction of crowds, refugee columns, and the physical devastation of Partition, elements that Singh could only describe through prose. The film necessarily condenses and simplifies the novel's interior monologues and ideological debates, particularly the reflections of Iqbal Singh and Hukum Chand.

I treat both the novel and the film as complementary texts that illuminate different dimensions of the Partition experience. While the novel offers psychological depth and symbolic complexity, the film provides visual immediacy and affective power. A comparative analysis of the two texts reveals how medium-specific conventions shape the representation of historical trauma.

## 1.5 Aim and objectives

The chapters that follow examine how Khushwant Singh's *Train to Pakistan* represents the trauma, violence, and communal disintegration caused by the Partition of 1947, and analyze how the 1998 film adaptation by Pamela Rooks reinterprets these themes for a visual medium. The study moves beyond surface-level plot analysis to investigate the novel's symbolic architecture, its critique of nationalism and administrative failure, and its portrayal of gendered violence.

The dissertation has five objectives:

1. Analyze how Partition reshaped Mano Majra from a place of relative harmony into a site of sectarian violence.
2. Examine the major themes: the train as symbol, religious identity, institutional failure, and gendered violence B_012B_014.
3. Evaluate Iqbal, Juggut, and Hukum Chand as different responses to moral crisis.
4. Compare the novel with the 1998 film adaptation — what the shift from page to screen changes or preserves.
5. Situate *Train to Pakistan* within Partition literature and its contribution to how South Asia remembers 1947.

## 1.6 Research methodology and scope

The analysis rests on close reading and thematic work, with film analysis and historical scholarship drawing on Gyanendra Pandey's argument that Partition was not just political but moral B_020. Joshi's work on the politics of feeling in Partition novels helps frame how affect operates across both texts B_021.

The primary texts are Khushwant Singh's novel *Train to Pakistan* (1956) and Pamela Rooks's film adaptation (1998). These texts are analyzed through a comparative framework that attends to both their thematic continuities and their medium-specific differences. The novel is read as a historical document that bears witness to the atrocities of 1947, while the film is examined as a visual reinterpretation that uses cinematic techniques to evoke affective responses.

I draw on secondary scholarship including scholarly articles and books on Partition literature, Sikh history, and the politics of communal identity. Joshi's analysis of affective politics in Partition novels provides a methodological point of departure: "This paper analyzes selected Indian partition novels to unravel affective politics underlined in them. The major affects underlying in these novels are those of love, hatred, happiness, unhappiness, and outrage" B_021. This dissertation extends such analyses by examining how affect operates across both literary and cinematic texts.

The study stays focused on Singh's novel and Rooks's film. While other Partition texts, such as Bhisham Sahni's *Tamas* and Salman Rushdie's *Midnight's Children*, offer valuable comparative contexts, the focus on *Train to Pakistan* allows for a sustained analysis of how a single narrative negotiates the complexities of Partition experience. The study does not claim to provide a comprehensive history of the Partition but rather to illuminate how literature and film mediate historical trauma.

## 1.7 Thesis statement

This dissertation argues that *Train to Pakistan* uses the small village of Mano Majra to show how quickly communal harmony breaks down when religious identity gets weaponized for political gain. The railroad, the three central characters, the complicity of local authorities, the gendered violence — all point to the same conclusion: Partition was not just a political line drawn on a map. It was a moral collapse. The 1998 film adaptation by Pamela Rooks takes this narrative into a visual register, trading the novel's symbolic density for cinematic immediacy. Comparing both texts, this study contends that Partition literature and film do not merely document what happened — they intervene in how South Asia continues to argue about identity, memory, and who belongs.
