## The Railroad as Symbol of Imperial Ambition and Colonial Rupture

Khushwant Singh's *Train to Pakistan* positions the railway as an ambivalent force threading through the Ganghetic plains, a structure that simultaneously promises connection and portends catastrophe. The railroad at Mano Majra represents the intrusive logic of a partitioned modernity, a line laid across a landscape whose social fabric had no space for such mechanical precision. Before the novel's central crisis erupts, the train station functions as a neutral landmark, a mundane site where Sikhs, Muslims, and Hindus share a village life that the tracks merely bisect without disrupting. Singh renders this equilibrium with deliberate restraint, allowing readers to register how thoroughly the railway had become absorbed into the rhythms of Punjabi agricultural existence even as it carried the weight of colonial administration and, later, the bureaucratic machinery of partition.

## The Ghost Train and the Epistemology of Violence

The arrival of the ghost train shatters this fragile equilibrium with devastating effect. When the special train steams into Mano Majra carrying only corpses, the locomotive ceases to function as a vehicle of transportation and becomes instead an instrument of revelation. The ghost train makes visible what the village had long managed to keep opaque: the scale of sectarian violence, the proximity of death, and the hollowness of the interfaith solidarity that the narrator has depicted as the village's defining character. Charyulu (2019, p. 47) observes that Singh employs the spectral train as a narrative device that collapses the distance between the village's moral self-image and the political reality pressing against it from outside. The train carries no passengers with agency, no cargo with exchange value, only the remains of those whose bodies have been rendered into evidence of a violence that has moved faster than any locomotive. This epistemological rupture proves more consequential than the violence itself, for it forces characters who had maintained a posture of communal equanimity to confront the limits of their collective indifference.

## The Transformation of Mano Majra: From Idyll to Threshold

The ghost train does not merely pass through Mano Majra; it leaves the village permanently altered, a community transformed from a site of agricultural rhythm and interfaith cohabitation into a threshold where the terms of belonging are suddenly negotiable. Before the train's arrival, the village had occupied a liminal position between local custom and national politics, buffered by its own obscurity. Afterward, that buffer dissolves. The railway station becomes a site where external forces demand a reckoning with communal identity, and the villagers discover that their long-practiced tolerance cannot withstand the systematic pressures that the train, in its ghostly apparition, announces. Singh conveys this transformation not through explicit declaration but through the changed weight of silence that settles over the village after the train departs. The railroad, once a symbol of the state's reach and modernity's intrusion, becomes in the novel's second half an instrument through which the community must negotiate its own survival, and the ghost train's shadow lingers over every subsequent decision the villagers make about flight, resistance, or complicity. The locomotive, in this reading, functions as both the mechanism of partition and the metaphor for its psychic cost, a presence whose passage through the village leaves the landscape forever marked by the political violence it carries.
