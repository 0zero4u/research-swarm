## The Ghost Train as Symbol of Partition Violence in *Train to Pakistan*

### The Railway as Narrative Axis

The railway occupies a central symbolic and structural position in Khushwant Singh's *Train to Pakistan*, functioning as the primary mechanism through which the violence of Partition is both literalized and metaphorized. The small fictional village of Mano Majra, positioned precisely on the India-Pakistan border, serves as an ideal setting for this exploration of communal catastrophe, with the railroad intersection acting as the conduit through which both peace and destruction flow [(363508_8461703, Charyulu 2019, p. 1)].

According to scholarly analysis of the novel, Mano Majra possesses "the sad differentiation of being a railroad intersection," yet the town "has no political or military significance" [(363508_8461703, Charyulu 2019, p. 1)]. This seemingly inconsequential position makes the village an ideal microcosm for examining how ordinary rural communities were drawn into the larger currents of sectarian violence. The railroad is described as "one of the town habitats, where women gather to talk and kids to play," suggesting the integral role this infrastructure played in daily Punjabi village life [(363508_8461703, Charyulu 2019, p. 2)].

### The Transformation of the Railroad

The arrival of the ghost trains marks a definitive transformation in the novel's atmosphere and the fate of its inhabitants. When trains begin arriving with "their grievous cargoes of death," the railroad ceases to be a site of quotidian activity and becomes instead a harbinger of catastrophe [(363508_8461703, Charyulu 2019, p. 2)]. Two significant trainloads of ravaged bodies arrive in Mano Majra, events that "uncover to the frightened townspeople that the Sikhs and Hindus are being slaughtered in Pakistan" [(363508_8461703, Charyulu 2019, p. 1)].
