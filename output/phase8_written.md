# Partition and Trauma in Khushwant Singh's *Train to Pakistan*

The historiography of the partition of the subcontinent reveals the creation of many different facets of society, as the events preceding and following the partition inflicted innumerable losses upon communities and individuals alike [PRI_001_C001, Priyanka Gupta, p.1]. The indelible marks left on the face of society were not easily removed, and the proposed partition of the country witnessed upheavals of unimaginable proportions on the eve of independence as communal riots engulfed the nation [PRI_001_C001, Priyanka Gupta, p.1]. These historical events exercised a deeper influence on the minds of the people, writers, and society at large, demonstrating how partition functioned not merely as a political event but as a transformative cultural phenomenon that reshaped literary expression in the subcontinent [PRI_001_C001, Priyanka Gupta, p.1].

Khushwant Singh's *Train to Pakistan* (1956) stands as both a symbolic and political novel within this literary tradition, where the train itself functions as a powerful symbol of movement and the mass exodus of people fleeing or being expelled during partition [PRI_001_C003, Priyanka Gupta, p.3]. The novel, along with Manohar Malgonkar's *A Bend in the Ganges* (1965), deals with the tragic theme of partition and the untold travails experienced by those caught in its wake [PRI_001_C002, Priyanka Gupta, p.2]. Both texts emerge as products of communal conflict, serving as literary outcomes that document and process the devastation wrought by partition upon the social fabric of the subcontinent [PRI_001_C002, Priyanka Gupta, p.2].

The enduring significance of these partition narratives lies in their representation of communal fights as the generative cause behind literary production, with both Singh and Malgonkar expressing the profound impact of partition on the societies they depict [PRI_001_C002, Priyanka Gupta, p.2]. Khushwant Singh, among the best-known writers at national and international levels, utilized his literary talents to create a work that captures not only the historical realities of partition but also its symbolic dimensions, positioning *Train to Pakistan* as a critical text for understanding how literature engages with one of the most traumatic events in South Asian history [PRI_001_C003, Priyanka Gupta, p.3].
